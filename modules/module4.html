Module 4: Accessible Media & Multimedia

Why does this matter?

One of the biggest gaps in accessibility is video and audio content. These are among the least accessible forms of content because they rely on multiple senses at once (hearing, vision, and cognitive processing). If one of those channels is not accessible (for example, if a user is blind), students can lose critical information.

If students cannot see or hear the original media, we need to ensure there is a way for them to perceive the same information through an equivalent form of access.

Key Idea: If it’s spoken, caption it. If it’s visual, describe it.

Spoken-only information should have captions or transcripts.

Visual-only information should have audio or text descriptions.

Captions

Captions for prerecorded videos are a Level A WCAG requirement and should be synchronized with the video. Effective captions should:

  • Appear at the same time the associated words are spoken (synchronized)
  • Be 99% accurate (not always the case for auto-generated captions)
  • Identify different speakers when necessary
  • Identify meaningful music or sound effects (e.g., boom!)
  • Capture emotions or emphasis (yelling or crying)
  • Use a readable sans serif font
  • Use no more than two lines with a maximum 45 characters per line at a time
  • Stay on screen for a minimum of 3 seconds

Transcripts

A transcript is the text of an entire video or audio recording, which does not need to be synchronized with the media itself. Transcripts are helpful for screen reader users and non-native speakers. They also allow quick review without having to rewatch the video or relisten to the audio. Effective transcripts should:

  • Contain all spoken words
  • Contain important words that appear on screen, such as titles and slide content
  • Identify different speakers when necessary
  • Identify meaningful music or sound effects
  • Capture emotions or emphasis (yelling or crying)
  • Use a readable sans serif font

Audio Descriptions (AD)

Audio descriptions explain important visual information that is not spoken.They are required when key information is only shown visually and when the narration alone doesn’t fully explain what’s happening. AD is particularly important for videos with graphs or charts or for theatrical content that involves important visual cues, movements, or positioning. Examples:

  • “The graph shows a sharp increase after 2020”
  • “Juliet stands on a balcony looking down at Romeo”

Making Lecture Recordings Accessible

To make your lecture recordings accessible, always provide captions or transcripts for audio and video recordings and verbally describe slides, charts, and actions. A major mistake is relying on visual-only explanations, which are not accessible to blind or low vision users. WCAG requires that visual information be available in another format (either audio or text). Videos should also avoid flashing colors, which could trigger seizures in epileptic viewers. Things to avoid:

  • Saying things like “as you can see here…”
  • No captions
  • Poor audio quality

Adding Captions in YouTube Studio

If you have recorded lecture videos without captions, a simple way to add captions is to upload the video to YouTube and enable auto-captions. YouTube auto captions do have some limitations as they are often inaccurate (with names, accents, technical terms), have missing punctuation or speaker changes, and do not include non-speech sounds.

Auto-captions do not meet accessibility standards unless edited, since captions must be highly accurate. However, they can be manually edited in YouTube Studio and reviewed for accuracy, timing, and speaker identification. It is also possible to upload your own caption file as a .srt or .vtt file.
