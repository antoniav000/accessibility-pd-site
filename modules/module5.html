Module 5: Inclusive Teaching Practices

Why Does this Module Matter?

In the last few modules, we covered ways in which we can make course content accessible through formatting. Accessibility is often treated as formatting (captions, PDFs, alt text), but in education, some of the biggest barriers are also structural and policy-based.

Inclusive teaching means designing flexibility into learning from the start, being explicit about inclusivity, removing unnecessary barriers in assessment and participation, and recognizing that fair does not always mean identical.

Stanford University presents an overview of inclusive teaching practices:

  • Providing fair and equitable access - Modules 2-4
  • Setting norms and commitments

Writing an Inclusive Syllabus

A syllabus sets the tone for the entire course experience. An inclusive statement signals that students are welcome to request accommodations and that accessibility is supported, not penalized.

Key components:

  • Invitation to communicate needs early
  • Commitment to flexibility where possible
  • Respect for disability services processes
  • Language that avoids burdening students

Flexible Deadlines & Participation Options

Having flexible deadline policies can also make the classroom more accessible. Rigid deadlines can disadvantage students with chronic illness, caregiving responsibilities, disability-related fluctuations, and mental health conditions.

Having flexible deadlines should not be inconvenient for the instructor, but should be something that is preemptively built into course design.

Examples of flexible deadlines and grading:

  • 24–48 hour grace periods for assignment due dates
  • “Best X of Y assignments count” grading
  • Alternative participation formats (discussion boards, reflections, etc.)

Flexibility should be built into course design, not handled as exceptions.

Accessible Assessments

Timed exams often measure speed more than learning, which can disadvantage:

  • Neurodivergent students
  • Students with processing differences
  • Students with test anxiety

Assessments should measure learning outcomes, not disability-related constraints.

Alternatives:

  • Untimed or extended-time assessments
  • Project-based evaluation
  • Open-book/open-note exams
  • Iterative assignments with feedback loops
  • Oral or multimodal assessments

Communicating About Accessibility with Students

How instructors talk about accessibility directly affects whether students feel safe disclosing needs.

Best practices:

  • Normalize accommodations early (don’t wait for crises)
  • Avoid language that frames accommodations as special treatment
  • Provide multiple channels for communication (email, forms, office hours)
  • Be explicit about what support looks like in your course

Foster Student Engagement & Community

An inclusive classroom is not only accessible in terms of materials and policies, but also in how students experience belonging, participation, and connection. Students are more likely to engage, persist, and succeed when they feel respected, represented, and supported within the learning environment.

Strategies for fostering an inclusive community:

  • Use discussion to increase engagement
  • Use prompts that elicit a wide range of perspectives
  • Assign roles to students for group work to ensure balanced participation
  • Use works or materials created by diverse writers or producers
  • Be mindful about putting students on the spot

Key Takeaway

Accessibility is not a set of accommodations added after the fact. It is a teaching philosophy that shapes how learning is designed from the beginning.

Inclusive teaching means anticipating variability in learners, building flexibility into structure, and removing unnecessary barriers before students encounter them.

Reflection Questions

  • How can course policies unintentionally create barriers for some students, even when the content itself is accessible?
  • Why is it important to build flexibility and accessibility into a course from the beginning rather than only responding to accommodation requests later?
  • Which inclusive teaching strategy from this module do you think would have the greatest impact on student learning and belonging, and why?
